Dr Satendra Singh

WHO IS Satendra

Dr. Satendra Singh is a medical doctor at Delhi's Guru Tegh Bahadur Hospital and the University College of Medical Sciences. He was a physiologist by profession when he acquired poliomyelitis at the age of nine months, but he went on to get a Bachelor of Medicine, Bachelor of Surgery, and eventually a Doctor of Medicine in Physiology at Ganesh Shankar Vidyarthi Memorial Medical College in Kanpur.

career

He is the first Indian to be awarded the renowned Henry Viscardi Achievement Awards, which recognise outstanding leaders in the global disability community. He is a well-known disability activist, well known for his tireless work to make public spaces accessible to disabled people, for which he received the President of India's National Award... He is also the first Indian to receive a fellowship from the University of Chicago's MacLean Center for Clinical Medical Ethics. Singh was a key figure in the development of epibatidine analogues, which led to the discovery of epiboxidine.

Unlocking Central posts for doctors with disabilities

The disabled fighter battled for the rights of not only himself, but also other disabled doctors. He also exposed UPSC's discrimination against doctors with impairments who applied for several Central Health Services (CHS) positions. In 2014, he was rejected, but he battled back and was eventually allowed to attend the interview. His application was again rejected in 2013, after he pointed out that the same discrimination existed for the other positions. He was given permission to request for a swift intervention. Doctors with impairments are not deemed eligible for specialist CHS posts in teaching, non-teaching, or public health expert cadres, according to his RTI. Undaunted, he complained once more, this time requesting that the health ministry allow all qualified doctors with disabilities to apply for these positions. Over the course of four years, his single-handed struggle for justice forced the Health Ministry to open 1,674 specialty central jobs for disabled doctors.

Medical council of india

He successfully advocated for the Central Government to change the controversial MCI guidelines, easing the prohibition on admission of candidates with specified disabilities, by filing a writ suit before the Supreme Court of India.

Rights in medical curriculum

He triggered a global discussion about the lack of disability rights in medical education and subsequently led an international partnership to develop disability competences for health professions instructors. Through persistent judicial campaigning with the Medical Council of India, he was able to get disability rights included as a human rights approach in India's new medical curriculum. This was a watershed moment because, unlike other medical curricula around the world, disability competencies that promote equality and social justice are now required in the Indian medical curriculum.

Accessibility to medical institutions

Government of India asked the Medical Council of India to provide guidelines to all medical institutions in India to be disabled-friendly as a consequence of his plea to the Court of the Chief Commissioner for Persons with Disabilities, under the Ministry of Social Justice and Empowerment. Despite these efforts, the majority of medical institutions have yet to follow the rules. Authorities at his medical college built ramps outside all of the hostels after his black armband protest on the United Nations' International Day of Persons with Disabilities in 2012.

Filing a case against a cabinet minister

He brought the first lawsuit under the new Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act, 2016, which imposes harsh penalties for violating the Act's provisions, against Satyadev Pachauri for publicly mocking a crippled employee. Pachauri, who is the minister of khadi and village industries in the Uttar Pradesh government, faces a six-month to five-year prison sentence if convicted.

Discriminatory checks

Dr Singh challenged the Union Public Service Commission’s format whereby disabled applicants were asked to paste photographs showing their disability as a proof. The Court of Chief Commissioner for Persons with Disabilities directed the UPSC to refrain from asking disabled candidates to submit photographs showing their disabilities and to consider the ‘permanent disability certificate’ issued from a government hospital as a valid proof. His advocacy led to the withdrawal of similar performa by IITJEE .

Dignified checking

Dr Singh approached the Directorate General of Civil Aviation (DGCA) and Bureau of Civil Aviation Security (BCAS) after being harassed by security personnel at the Hyderabad airport because of his orthosis. He requested that the rustic screening procedures and guidelines be amended, taking into account the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities. Both the DGCA and BCAS have been urged by the disability watchdog to ensure that individuals with disabilities are not harassed or humiliated at airports, and that security staff are aware of their rights.

Wrong celebration

World Polio Day (October 24) was founded by Rotary International to commemorate the birthday of virologist Jonas Salk, who invented the polio vaccine. Dr. Singh questioned this incorrect practice in a paper published in the official journal of the Edward Jenner Society's Vaccine (journal). Salk's 100th birthday was commemorated with a Google doodle, which confirmed his birthday as October 28. Despite media reports to the contrary, Rotary continues to commemorate the day on October 24.His tireless advocacy resulted in the installation of ramps at ATMs.He also used the Right to Information Act to raise awareness of inaccessible ATMs and post offices in the city for those with disabilities.

Intersex rights

Dr. Singh, together with his colleagues Dr. Aqsa Shaikh and Dr. Sanjay Sharma, petitioned the Delhi Commission for Protection of Child Rights, which recommended to the Delhi government that medically unnecessary sex normalisation procedures on intersex children be banned.
